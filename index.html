The March 19 Process

How democratic competition is being crushed in Türkiye

A visual report on the democratic backsliding after March 19, 2025

Prepared and published by March 19 Platform in July 2026

Democracy
requires
more than
elections

Democracy is not defined by voting alone. It requires free debate, lawful courts, peaceful political competition, independent institutions, free media, and the genuine possibility that power can change hands through elections.

Türkiye is a NATO ally, a G20 economy, and a strategically important country linking Europe, the Black Sea, the Middle East, and the Eastern Mediterranean. Democratic change in Türkiye therefore has consequences far beyond its borders.

Over the past decade, democratic backsliding in Türkiye has become increasingly visible. Independent institutions have weakened, political competition has grown less equal, space for dissent has steadily narrowed. But the events that began on March 19, 2025 marked a decisive turning point on this path.

This booklet explains why. The March 19 Process was not simply about the detention of one opposition leader, but about reshaping the rules of political competition in, and ultimately the future of, Türkiye.

What is the
March 19 Process?

From authoritarian competition to a civilian coup

For years, political competition in Türkiye became increasingly unequal. State resources, media dominance, and growing pressure on opposition parties steadily tilted the playing field in favor of President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan. Yet the opposition recovered, winning the 2024 local elections and emerging as the country's largest political force.

On March 19, 2025, Istanbul Mayor Ekrem İmamoğlu (the opposition's leading presidential contender) and more than 100 politicians, municipal officials, advisers, and associates were detained. What followed extended far beyond one criminal investigation.

The March 19 Process marked a decisive turning point. Rather than merely defeating the opposition at the ballot box, it seeks to dismantle the opposition's capacity to compete at all. The emerging model resembles today's Russia, where elections still take place but no longer offer a realistic path to political change. For this reason, many in Türkiye describe March 19 as a civilian coup against democratic politics.

The goal:

Neutralize organized political opposition before the next national election.

The method:

Investigations, detentions, legal restrictions, administrative intervention, and sustained political pressure.

The turning point:

March 19, 2025 — The detention of Istanbul Mayor Ekrem İmamoğlu and more than 100 others.

The risk:

A political system in which elections continue, but only for show, and genuine competition disappears.

March 19 was not one case.
It marked a new stage in the erosion of democratic competition.

Same Envelope, Different Fate

The 2019 Istanbul election produced one of the most controversial decisions in modern Turkish politics. Although voters cast four separate local-election ballots in a single envelope, the Supreme Election Council annulled only the metropolitan mayoral ballot (the one won by opposition candidate Ekrem İmamoğlu) while accepting the other three as valid. Authorities cited procedural irregularities, yet no evidence of organized fraud emerged from subsequent investigations. The decision was widely seen as political rather than legal. When the election was repeated, İmamoğlu defeated the ruling party's candidate by an even larger margin.

The first test of whether election results would be respected.
When the ballot box survived, other methods followed.

2019–2024:
Pressure on opposition municipalities

After losing Türkiye's largest cities in 2019, Erdoğan's government sought to ensure it would not happen again.

Rather than accepting the new political balance, it subjected opposition-run municipalities to years of financial, administrative, legal, and political pressure, to portray them as ineffective and incapable of governing.

Yet the strategy failed. Opposition mayors continued to deliver public services, expanded their public support, and in 2024 the CHP became Türkiye's largest political party nationwide, first time since Erdoğan took office.

Under Turkish law, presidential candidates must hold a university degree. Just days after Ekrem İmamoğlu formally declared his intention to run for president, his university diploma, issued in 1994, was annulled in 2025 by a university board that critics argue lacked the legal authority to make such a decision. This effectively prevented him from meeting one of the constitutional requirements for the presidency.

The following day, he was detained.

The goal was no longer to win the next election.
It was to shape who could compete in it.

When legal process
becomes
political pressure.

The March 19 Process did not rely on a single case. It combined dozens of investigations, overlapping prosecutions, prolonged detention, and legal uncertainty into a sustained form of political pressure.

Political pressure Not just
a prosecution,
a pattern.

Legal tools used against Ekrem İmamoğlu

1. 2,430+ years

Combined prison sentences sought across dozens of cases against Ekrem İmamoğlu.

2. Dozens of investigations

Investigations have ranged from corruption and terrorism allegations to highly technical municipal procedures, including claims about construction measurements and procurement decisions.

3. The “Ahmak” case

In December 2022, İmamoğlu was sentenced to prison and a political ban after repeating Interior Minister Süleyman Soylu's own “fool” remark back to him. The verdict remains under appeal.

4. Lawyers targeted

Lawyers representing İmamoğlu and other officials have been subjected to searches, detentions, and investigations for their professional activities.

5. Municipal staff targeted

Operations increasingly targeted not only elected officials but also senior municipal administrators and technical staff, disrupting the day-to-day functioning of local government.

6. Overlapping cases

Multiple investigations were pursued simultaneously, creating continuous legal uncertainty even when earlier cases remained unresolved.

Many defendants spent extended periods in detention before indictments were finalized, while investigations often expanded faster than courts could review earlier cases.

How legal pressure
was sustained.

The March 19 Process was not sustained by a single indictment. It was maintained through repeated mechanisms that prolonged uncertainty, shaped public perception, and kept pressure on political actors over time.

Not an isolated case.
A continuous pattern.

Key mechanisms that sustained legal pressure

1. Pressure to become a witness

Some detainees reported being offered release or lighter treatment in exchange for statements implicating other suspects.

2. Prolonged pre-trial detention

Many detainees remained imprisoned for months before indictments were completed or trials began.

3. Broad and weakly supported allegations

Many indictments relied heavily on anonymous witnesses, hearsay, or indirect claims rather than publicly presented material evidence.

4. Overlapping investigations

New investigations often began before earlier cases concluded, extending legal uncertainty indefinitely.

5. Media leaks

Confiscated phones, private conversations and investigation materials frequently appeared in pro-government media before being examined in court.

6. Legal pressure as a process

Rather than a single prosecution, multiple simultaneous investigations created continuous legal, political and psychological pressure.

Beyond the courtroom

For many detainees, imprisonment marked the beginning—not the end—of a broader process affecting their families, health, livelihoods and daily lives.

Family

  • Families are separated for months or years.
  • Detainees are transferred to prisons hundreds of miles away.
  • Relatives travel for hours—sometimes 8–12 hours each way—for short visits.
  • Threats against detainees often target their spouses, children or elderly parents.

Employment & livelihoods

  • Salaries are suspended.
  • Many are dismissed from their jobs.
  • Household income disappears overnight.
  • Families are forced to sell homes, cars and savings to survive.

Health

  • Access to adequate medical care is delayed or denied.
  • Malnutrition and stress worsen existing conditions.
  • Many detainees lose significant weight.

  • Held in pre-trial detention despite serious medical conditions.
  • He has lost more than 20 kg (44 lbs) since his detention.
  • Reports indicate risk of life-threatening complications.
  • Medical requests have been repeatedly delayed.

Privacy & dignity

  • Phones and computers are confiscated.
  • Private messages and photos are leaked to pro-government media.
  • Smear campaigns are used to publicly destroy reputations.

The conditions of detention

Systematic practices inside prisons raise serious human rights concerns.

1. Overcrowding

  • Prisons are operating far beyond capacity.
  • Cells designed for two or three people often hold many more.
  • Many detainees are forced to sleep on floors, mattresses or with no bedding.

2. Isolation & solitary confinement

  • Detainees are subjected to isolation for long periods to break their will.
  • Prolonged isolation causes severe psychological harm.

3. Strip searches

  • Detainees—including women—are subjected to strip searches.
  • These practices are degrading, humiliating and violate basic human dignity.
  • Such searches are used routinely, even without any concrete security justification.

4. Health & medical neglect

  • Medical care is delayed or denied. Chronic illnesses worsen.
  • Malnutrition and weight loss are widespread.
  • Multiple serious medical conditions—such as in the case of Murat Çalık—are ignored.
  • Significant weight loss and risk of life-threatening complications are common.
  • Requests for release for treatment are ignored.

5. Pressure & threats

  • Detainees are pressured to give false statements.
  • Prosecutors threaten detainees with harm to their children or elderly relatives.
  • Such pressure undermines fair trial rights and the right to remain silent.

6. Transfers to distant prisons

  • Detainees are transferred to prisons far from their families.
  • Visits become costly, exhausting and extremely difficult.
  • These transfers isolate detainees and punish families.

7. Unfair co-detention

  • Many pre-trial detainees—who have not been convicted—are placed in the same wards as convicted criminals.
  • Including those sentenced for ordinary offenses.
  • This practice increases risk, pressure and psychological harm.

8. Surveillance & leaks

  • Phones and personal devices are confiscated.
  • Private communications are monitored or leaked to pro-government media.
  • Private messages and photos of detainees and their families are published to discredit and intimidate.

Pre-trial detention became a lived experience, not just a legal status.

These practices violate Türkiye's constitution and international human rights obligations.

Main opposition under attack

Since the 2024 local elections, the Republican People's Party (CHP) has become Türkiye's largest political party, representing millions of voters across the country. Instead of seeking to regain public support through electoral competition, the government has intensified judicial and administrative pressure on the party, targeting its mayors, leadership, municipalities, and organizational structure.

The “absolute nullity” case

On May 21, 2026, a court lacking jurisdiction issued a ruling of “absolute nullity” to annul the 38th Ordinary Congress held three years earlier in 2023.

  • At that congress, no one expected it at the time, the challenger candidate defeated the long-time leader and a young, reformist team took over.
  • Within months, CHP became the first party in the country in the local elections.
  • When the March 19 Process failed to bring CHP to its knees, the government turned to the courts to remove the elected leadership.
  • The court declared the 2023 congress void—erasing the will of the delegates—and imposed the return of the old leadership to the party.
